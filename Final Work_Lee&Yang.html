II. Sentence and Chapter Length Comparison¶

We further broke down each chapter into sentences. First, we counted the number of space in each chapter to obtain the approximate number of words. The initial codes we used before the presentation to count the number of words is [len(s) for s in huck_finn_chapters], which actually counted the number of characters in each chapter and gave us much larger average sentence length than it should be. After we realized the number was so off, we made some changes on our original codes and used count() function to count the number of “ ” (empty space) in each chapter. Then, we counted the number of sentences by counting the number of appearance of comma, period and exclamation marks and added them together to obtain the total number of sentences in each chapter. Later, we used the number of words divided by the total number of sentences to obtain the average sentence length in each chapter.

IV. Emotion Words and Story Plot¶

One question we had was why Mark Twain used different styles of writing between $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$ and $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$. We hypothesize that this is due to the fact that Mark Twain didn’t expect $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$ to be such a huge success. In his former work, Mark Twain flippantly refers to himself as the narrator throughout the book. In fact, through the guise of the narrator, he even compliments himself. This fact shows us that he took the writing of $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$ not as seriously as he took $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$. Most likely, after seeing the massive success of his first work, he took the writing of $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$ much more seriously.

IV. Word Tokenization¶

After having analyzed the general emotion mood in both works, we wished to delve into these two books more specifically by doing word and special character analysis to explore more interesting patterns.

The Tokenizer package gave us the ability to break down one sentence into words or special characters. Here, we built a function called tok(), which can break down the whole novel into separate words and special characters. We then used counter() function to count the number of frequency of each word or character apperance and rank them in descending order. We selected out the top 30 most frequent characters and words and further plotted them into bar charts for convenient comparison.

In [22]:
def tok(text: str) -> Union[List[str], Set[str], Dict[str, int]]:
        return Counter([t.txt for t in tokenize(text) if t.txt])

We ran our code to find frequently used words in $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$ that weren’t used in $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$, and vice versa. What we found was that $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$ uses a lot of words referring to objects, such as “Canoe” and “Cabin”. One possible explanation for this is because $\textit{Huckleberry Finn}$’s storyline revolves a lot around survival while on the run, and the aforementioned words are common objects that have been used in such instances in their territory. $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$ is about adventure as well, but not as much about survival. Obviously, the two con men Huck and Jim let on their raft pretend to be a “Duke” and a "King", which doesn’t happen in $\textit{Tom Sawyer}$.
